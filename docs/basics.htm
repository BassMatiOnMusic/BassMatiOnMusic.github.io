Notes, Intervals, Scales

In western music, we have seven notes, A, B, C, D, E, F and G. The distance between two notes is called interval. The length of an interval is measured in half-tone steps. The distance between the notes varies, most are two half-steps apart, some are one half-step apart:

NOTE
The intervals B–C and E–F are one half step long.

The octave is a special interval. It is 12 half-steps long, and the frequency of the higher note is exactly double the frequency of the lower note, and they both have the same name (A in the image above). After 12 half-steps, the scale is repeated like the hours on the clock:

Sometimes the octave number is included in the note name to indicate the absolute pitch, and for some obscure reason octave numbers change on the C, not A, as one might guess. Such qualified note names are called absolute note names. In contrast, notes without octave numbers are called relative note names or just note names.

The distribution of notes over the octave is called the scale, and is the source of a scale's harmonic potential.

In a technical sense, the diminished seventh is actually a major 6th, but in certain (dim7)chords, a seventh is required by the chord naming conventions.

Note that the A minor scale can be obtained by rotating the C major scale clockwise by three half-steps. They share the same notes, so they are called parallel scales.

Triads

If more than one notes sound at the same time, we speak of dyads (2), triads (3), or chords (4 or more). Sometimes they are commonly referred to as "chords".

Notes in chords are not put randomly together. Triads and chord are build from thirds, stacked on top of each other. Because we have two flavours of thirds, minor and major, we can build four different triads, here on based on the root note C:

MEMORIZE
Minor and major triads are the most common. The minor triad has a minor 3rd, the major triad has a major 3rd interval.

Triads are named after the root note, followed by the triad variant name. The C major triad is an exception: Its official name is "C triad", "C major" is sometimes spoken in the context of a triad but never written, because the "M" has a different meaning in the context of chords and could lead to confusion.

Chord Naming Conventions

Chord names are built with efficiency and readability in mind. As a result, names sometimes don't appear to be logical – just like written sequences of letters are spoken in a 'strange' way. Nevertheless, there is a set of rules:

  1. The first letter is the note name, optionally followed by accidentals.
  2. If the chord has a minor 3rd interval, a lowercase "m" follows the note name.
  3. Minor intervals except the 7th are preceded by a lowercase "m", major intervals have no prefix.
  4. A major 7th interval is preced by an uppercase "M", the minor 7th has no prefix.
  5. Alterations such as ♭5 follow.
  6. Extensions are appended.

Sharps and Flats

You may have noticed that there are "empty spaces" between the named notes. In fact these are notes, too, and they are all equally important.
